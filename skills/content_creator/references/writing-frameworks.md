# Writing Frameworks Reference

Use this file when generating content. Each section maps to a specific framework with its core principles. The SKILL.md tells you which framework(s) to apply for each platform — come here for the actual techniques.

---

## 1. Smart Brevity
*Best for: LinkedIn posts, internal updates, short-form announcements*

- **The "A-ha" Headline**: Open with a punchy, bolded lead-in that captures the single most important idea. It should make the reader stop.
- **One Big Thing**: Identify the single most important takeaway and surface it immediately — before any context or background.
- **Why It Matters**: Explicitly state the significance in one sentence. Don't make the reader infer it.
- **Axioms**: Use bullets and bold text to allow for "skimmability." Readers scan before they read.

**Applying it**: Write the "Why It Matters" sentence first as your anchor, then build the rest of the post around it. If you can't state why it matters in one sentence, the idea isn't sharp enough yet.

## 2. Made to Stick (SUCCESs Framework)
*Best for: Blog articles, WeChat 公众号, any content meant to be remembered*

- **Simple**: Find the *core* of the idea — the single most essential insight stripped of everything else. If you had to tattoo the idea on someone's wrist, what would it say?
- **Unexpected**: Surprise the reader to grab and hold attention. Find the counterintuitive angle — what do most people believe that isn't quite right?
- **Concrete**: Use sensory, specific language. Avoid abstractions. "Response time dropped from 4 hours to 2 minutes" beats "significantly improved response time" every time.
- **Credible**: Build trust through vivid, specific details or "anti-authorities" (real people, real numbers, real situations). Statistics work when they're surprising; stories work when they're specific.
- **Emotional**: Make the reader feel something. Answer "WIIFY" — What's In It For You. Connect the idea to something the reader already cares about.
- **Stories**: Use narratives to give the reader a "mental flight simulator." A story lets them experience the idea, not just understand it.

**Applying it**: Start with Simple (what's the core?), then find the Unexpected angle (what's the twist?), then make it Concrete (what's the specific example?). The other three follow naturally.

## 3. On Writing Well
*Best for: Blog articles, WeChat 公众号, any long-form content*

- **Clutter Control**: Strip every sentence to its cleanest components. Delete adverbial clutter ("very", "really", "basically", "actually"). If a word doesn't add meaning, cut it.
- **Unity**: Maintain a single point of view, one tense, and one consistent mood throughout the piece. Decide upfront: who is speaking, to whom, and why.
- **The Lead**: The first sentence must hook the reader — it's an audition for the rest of the piece. The last sentence of each paragraph must "tease" the next, pulling the reader forward.
- **The Ending**: End with a single sentence or image that resonates. Don't summarise — land. The final line should feel inevitable in hindsight.

**Applying it**: Write a first draft freely. Then edit ruthlessly: read every sentence aloud and cut anything that slows you down. Pay extra attention to the first paragraph and the last line of each section.

## 4. StoryBrand
*Best for: Any content where you want readers to see themselves taking action — especially blog intros, LinkedIn posts about transformation, and WeChat pieces about adopting new approaches*

- **The Hero Rule**: The *reader* is the hero of the story — not you, not your company, not the technology. You are the Guide (think Yoda, not Luke). Write from that position: you've been where they are, you know the path, you're here to help them succeed.
- **The Villain**: Name the specific problem the reader is facing. Readers engage when they feel understood. The villain isn't a person — it's the friction, inefficiency, confusion, or missed opportunity standing between them and their goal.
- **The Plan**: Give the reader 3 clear, simple steps to solve their problem. Complexity creates hesitation. Simplicity creates confidence.
- **High Stakes**: Clearly articulate what becomes possible when the reader succeeds. Frame the positive vision, not the threat. (Note for this author: always frame stakes as possibility gained, not loss avoided.)

**Applying it**: Before writing, answer: Who is the hero? What villain (problem) are they fighting? What 3-step plan do I offer? What does success look like for them? Build the content around those answers.

## 5. Everybody Writes
*Best for: Social posts, X/Twitter threads, Xiaohongshu, any web content*

- **The Ugly First Draft (TUFD)**: Focus on the idea first — write badly if you have to. Then "edit for empathy" in the second pass: read it as the reader, not the writer.
- **Customer-Centric**: Replace "I" and "We" with "You" as much as possible. The reader's world is what matters. Every sentence should be answering an implicit question the reader has.
- **Default to Short**: If a sentence can be shorter, it should be. If a paragraph can be one sentence, let it be one sentence. Brevity is a courtesy to the reader.
- **Edit for Empathy**: In your second pass, ask: Is this clear? Does this respect the reader's time? Would a real person find this useful or just impressive-sounding?

**Applying it**: Write a full draft first (TUFD). Then do a second pass specifically asking "Is this about me, or is this about the reader?" Shift every sentence toward the reader's perspective.

## 6. The Copywriter's Handbook
*Best for: All headlines, hook lines, first tweets, LinkedIn first lines, Xiaohongshu titles*

- **The 4 U's**: Every headline or hook should be:
  - **Urgent**: Give the reader a reason to engage *now*, not later
  - **Unique**: Say something they haven't heard before, or say it in a way they haven't heard
  - **Ultra-specific**: Vague headlines are ignored. Specific numbers, situations, and outcomes earn attention. "20 minutes" beats "less time". "$120k" beats "significant savings".
  - **Useful**: Make it clear the reader will gain something valuable by continuing
- **Benefits Over Features**: Don't describe what something *is* — describe what it *does for the reader*. "AI that handles 500 conversations" is a feature. "Your team gets to focus on the work that actually matters" is a benefit.
- **The "Bribe"**: Offer immediate value or a clear hook to keep the reader going. The reader is always asking "what's in this for me?" — answer it before they have to ask.

**Applying it**: Write your hook, then score it against the 4 U's. If it's missing two or more, rewrite it. The most common miss is Ultra-specific — add a concrete number or situation and watch the hook sharpen immediately.
